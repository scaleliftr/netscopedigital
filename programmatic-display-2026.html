Programmatic display in 2026: what's actually working.

The programmatic ecosystem has been through four identity crises in five years. Cookie deprecation, MFA sites, header bidding consolidation, generative creative. Here's a practitioner view of what's profitable in 2026, what's been hollowed out, and what to stop paying for.

Published February 22, 2026·By the Netscope Campaign Team

If you ran programmatic display in 2019 and you ran it again today using the same playbook, your recovered ROAS would be somewhere between half and a quarter of what it was then. The cost of access has gone up, the signal you have to target with has eroded, and the inventory supply has become a minefield of made‑for‑advertising sites. The good news is that the practitioners who've adjusted are performing better than ever, because the bar is now much easier to clear.

Here's what's working.

1. Supply Path Optimisation (SPO) is now table stakes

A typical programmatic chain in 2019 had your DSP bid through 12–15 different SSP paths to reach the same impression on the same publisher. Every intermediary took a cut. The IAB's ads.txt cleaned up the worst of it, but the industry still bids wastefully.

A disciplined SPO setup in 2026 collapses this to one direct path per publisher — usually the publisher's preferred SSP — and blocks everything else. The impression cost drops 15–25% without any other change, because you stop paying three middlemen for the same eyeball. This is the single biggest single‑change efficiency gain available to most display programmes right now.

2. Private marketplace (PMP) and programmatic guaranteed, not open exchange

The open exchange is still where most impressions are traded. It's also where the lion's share of fraud, MFA inventory and brand‑unsafe placements live. For retargeting — where the user is pre‑qualified and you're trying to convert on brand equity — running exclusively against PMPs and programmatic guaranteed deals with premium publishers moves the needle more than any bidding tactic.

Yes, CPMs are higher. Yes, coverage is lower. No, that doesn't matter, because on a CPA model what matters is converted sales per dollar spent, and premium inventory converts at multiples of open‑exchange inventory.

3. Attention metrics over viewability

Viewability — the MRC standard of "50% of pixels in view for 1 second" — was always a low bar, and in 2026 it's a floor, not a target. Attention metrics (time‑in‑view, scroll velocity, in‑focus signals from vendors like Adelaide and Lumen) actually correlate to outcomes, and they're worth the 3–5% measurement overhead.

If your programme is still optimising to viewability percentage and not to attention‑adjusted CPM, you are paying premium rates for premium‑looking inventory that is not being seen.

4. MFA exclusion lists, refreshed monthly

Made‑for‑advertising sites — low‑quality content farms with ten ad slots per page — have been estimated to absorb 15–20% of all open exchange spend. Every DSP now offers MFA exclusion, but the lists go stale quickly. Refresh them monthly, cross‑reference Jounce Media's list, and audit your inventory reports for any domain delivering more than 2% of impressions that you can't verify as legitimate.

5. Cookieless identifiers as the default, not the backup

The transition narrative has been "prepare for cookieless," as if cookies were still the default and cookieless was the fallback. In 2026 the inversion has happened. On Safari and Firefox there is no third‑party cookie. On iOS most in‑app identifiers have been opted out. On Chrome, what cookies remain are increasingly rate‑limited and watched by Privacy Sandbox signals.

The identifiers that matter in 2026:

  • First‑party cookies on your own domain — still the foundation
  • UID 2.0 — The Trade Desk's hashed‑email identifier, widely adopted across demand and supply
  • RampID (LiveRamp) — the enterprise equivalent, strong in retail
  • Publisher‑first‑party IDs — PMP deals with logged‑in audience data
  • Topics API (Privacy Sandbox) — worth testing for prospecting, still immature for retargeting

Stop treating any of these as experimental. They are the stack now.

6. Dynamic creative optimisation, driven by product feed

The oldest trick in retargeting and somehow the most under‑used. Your creative should be generated from your product feed, in real time, with the exact item the shopper looked at, current price, current availability, and a CTA that matches where they abandoned. Not a static banner, not a template with the product image dropped in, a truly dynamic template that renders at bid time.

Brands still running static banner templates against their retargeting pool are leaving 20–30% of conversions on the table against the same audience with DCO. The production cost is real but paid back within weeks in a CPA model.

7. Generative creative — selectively

Yes, the tools are good enough to produce display creative now. No, they are not good enough to produce good creative for your brand without human art direction. Our default stance in 2026: AI‑generated lifestyle backgrounds and pattern variations for DCO, human‑art‑directed hero frames and typography, always with a brand review step before anything renders on a publisher.

The brands using generative well are using it for variation and velocity — 200 SKU‑level creative variants instead of 20. The brands using it badly are using it to replace a creative team, and it shows.

What to stop paying for

  1. Viewability‑only optimisation — you are buying pixels, not attention
  2. Open exchange as primary path — the long‑tail quality has collapsed
  3. Demographic targeting overlays on top of retargeting — you already have the user, you don't need to pay an extra layer to "confirm" they're 25–34
  4. Third‑party audience segments on retargeting campaigns — same reason
  5. DSPs that don't let you run SPO properly — there are plenty that do

The meta point

Programmatic display in 2026 rewards operators who run fewer, cleaner paths against better‑identified audiences with better creative. It punishes operators who treat it as a volume game against cheap impressions. The industry's long slide toward MFA inventory and wasted impressions was a brief moment of arbitrage that ended somewhere around mid‑2024. If your display programme feels like it's been getting worse, that's why.

Fixing it is not hard. It's just unglamorous: tighten the supply path, exclude the junk, measure attention, run a real DCO, use identifiers that survive Safari. The operators doing that are having a better year than they've had since 2019.
