Why your cart abandonment recovery strategy is losing money.

Industry cart abandonment sits around 70%. Most brands try to fix it with one email an hour after the session ended and a second the next morning. That covers maybe a fifth of the audience. Here's where the other four‑fifths of recoverable revenue actually lives.

Published April 14, 2026·By the Netscope Campaign Team

If your cart abandonment strategy is "we send two emails from Klaviyo and run Meta dynamic ads," you have a cart abandonment strategy roughly as sophisticated as your competitors in 2018. The reason you're leaving money on the table is not that email is broken — it's that email only reaches the shoppers who gave you a working address and opened your last campaign. For a mid‑sized retailer, that's maybe 18–25% of the abandoning audience.

The other 75% are the shoppers we get paid to recover. Below is the 72‑hour playbook we run for fashion and marketplace brands on CPA — what channel fires when, what creative it uses, and why.

Minute 0 to 60 — the hot window

The single highest‑converting hour in all of retargeting is the hour immediately after a shopper leaves the cart. Conversion rate on a retargeting ad served in minute 0–60 is roughly 3–4× what it is in the same ad served 24 hours later. The shopper is still considering. They haven't switched tasks. The item is still in working memory.

This is where web push earns its keep. If the shopper has opted in — and 12–18% of sessions on a well‑implemented push prompt do — you can send a single, product‑specific notification within 15 minutes of abandonment. No cookie required, no email needed, no creative production bottleneck. The open rate on these will sit in the 6–9% range, and roughly a third of opens convert.

In parallel, the display pixel starts bidding on impressions across open exchanges. The first few impressions land on the shopper's next site visit, usually within the same hour. We cap frequency at 3 in the first 60 minutes. Any more than that and you're past the point of persuasion, into the territory of annoyance.

Hour 1 to 24 — the decision window

By hour 3, most of the "I was just interrupted" recoveries have already converted. What's left is the shopper who had a real reason to hesitate — price, shipping, a size concern, a competing option in another tab. This is when the message needs to stop being a reminder and start being an answer.

The creative shift is subtle but important: swap the raw product card for a card that adds one piece of information. Free shipping threshold. Return window. A lifestyle image that shows scale. A star rating pulled from Trustpilot or Yotpo. You are no longer saying "you left this in your cart." You are answering the question the shopper was actually asking when they left.

For push, the 12‑hour follow‑up is where segmentation matters most. A shopper who abandoned on the product detail page gets a different message than one who abandoned at checkout. The former is still deciding if they want the item; the latter already decided and something else got in the way. Treating them identically is a common and costly mistake.

Hour 24 to 72 — the discount question

Somewhere in this window the temptation to reach for a 10% off code becomes overwhelming, because the numbers say it works. Two thoughts before you do it:

  • A promo code trained into a retargeting sequence becomes a permanent line item on your P&L. Shoppers learn that abandoning the cart triggers a discount, and abandonment becomes the price — which is a margin problem, not a conversion problem.
  • Free shipping is almost always more efficient than a percentage‑off discount of equivalent value, because it protects unit margin and feels bigger than it costs.

If you must use a promo, make it time‑limited (36 hours), make it unavailable on the first‑touch creative, and suppress it from anyone who has redeemed one in the last 30 days. And please don't put the code in the push notification preview — half the recipients will take the code without ever opening.

Day 3 to day 14 — the long tail

After 72 hours, the probability of recovery drops off a cliff. But it doesn't go to zero, and the long tail is where a well‑run programme separates itself from a mediocre one. Three things we keep running in this window:

  1. Restock and price‑change triggers. If the item went back in stock, or the price dropped, that's a new piece of information and the shopper hasn't seen it. Push delivers this beautifully.
  2. Related‑category retargeting. The shopper looked at a navy wool coat and didn't buy. Show them navy wool coats from other brands in your catalogue. Don't keep hitting them with the same item.
  3. Category exit suppression. If they bought something in the same category from you in the last 14 days, stop retargeting them. This should be obvious. It often isn't.

What a complete 72‑hour flow looks like

For a typical fashion retailer we run this structure end to end:

  • Minute 0–15: push notification (if opted in), product‑specific, no discount
  • Minute 0–60: display retargeting, frequency cap 3, product‑specific dynamic creative
  • Hour 1: email 1 (the one you already have)
  • Hour 3–12: display rotates to "answer the objection" creative — shipping, returns, rating
  • Hour 12: push follow‑up, segmented by abandonment stage
  • Hour 24: email 2
  • Hour 24–72: display with social proof + free shipping over threshold
  • Day 3+: long‑tail push on restock, price drop, related category
  • Post‑conversion: suppress immediately across all channels

Run this correctly and cart‑recovery revenue typically adds 8–14% to site revenue in the first 90 days, with incremental margin intact. It's not magic — it's just more surface area than your email tool alone can give you.

The mistake we see most often

More than any single tactical error, the mistake that costs brands the most is treating retargeting as a performance lever and nothing else. It isn't. It's the channel the shopper sees after their highest‑intent session of the month. Whatever creative you run there, whatever tone, whatever offer — it becomes part of the brand experience. Cheap, repetitive, visibly automated ads in this window undo the brand work that got them to the site in the first place.

Spend ten more minutes on the creative than you think you need to.
