The 7 types of shoppers you can win back with retargeting.

Treating every abandoner the same is why so many retargeting programmes plateau. The shopper who left at the PDP is in a different psychological place than the one who left at the payment screen. Here's how we segment them, and the creative we run for each.

Published March 10, 2026·By the Netscope Campaign Team

When we onboard a new retailer, the first thing we do is sit with their analytics and segment the last 90 days of non‑converting sessions. Not by demographics, not by source — by what the shopper did on the site and where they left. That's the signal that tells you what they were thinking and what will move them.

These seven segments cover roughly 95% of recoverable sessions across the retailers we've worked with. They're in rough order of recoverability.

1. The interrupted buyer

Behaviour: reached checkout, filled in payment details, did not submit. Session ended within 60 seconds.

What's going on: a tab closed, a phone rang, a card was declined and they meant to come back. Almost nothing changed their mind about the purchase.

What works: a single push notification within 15 minutes — "Your order is waiting" — linking straight back to the populated cart. No discount, no persuasion. Just the path back. Recovery on this segment is often 30–45% of the audience.

2. The price hesitator

Behaviour: repeated sessions viewing the same product, maybe added to cart, left without buying.

What's going on: they want the item, they're weighing it against the budget. Probably checking competitor pricing.

What works: free shipping over their AOV threshold. A star rating to justify the price. Price‑drop alerts via push if the item moves. Don't lead with a percentage discount — it trains the behaviour and you'll pay it forever. Save the discount for day 4+.

3. The size‑or‑fit concerned

Behaviour: viewed a PDP, opened the size guide, viewed reviews filtered by fit, left without adding to cart.

What's going on: the item passed the style test. They don't trust that it will fit or look right.

What works: retargeting creative that shows the product on multiple body types, user‑generated content pulled from Instagram, the return policy front and centre. This segment is the best argument for free returns existing.

4. The comparison shopper

Behaviour: viewed 4+ products in the same category across a session or two, added and removed from cart, used category filters heavily.

What's going on: they've decided to buy something in this category but haven't decided which thing.

What works: category retargeting, not product retargeting. Show them a rotating creative with your top 6 products in the category, a bestseller badge, differentiators like sustainable materials or limited edition. Do not hammer them with the specific item they last viewed — they've already rejected it.

5. The window shopper

Behaviour: single session, browsed 2–5 products, no add‑to‑cart, no account, bounced from the homepage or category.

What's going on: they're not in the market yet. They're building a mental shortlist for later.

What works: brand‑building display. Lifestyle imagery, editorial content, the origin story. You are not closing this sale in the next 24 hours. You are making sure they remember you when intent arrives in three weeks. A small, consistent frequency beats a large burst here.

6. The deal hunter

Behaviour: landed via a promo page, a voucher site, or a paid search term containing "coupon/discount/sale." Viewed sale items only.

What's going on: they shop on discount, not on brand. They will convert on a promotion and not otherwise.

What works: segment them into your sale flow, retarget them with dynamic sale creative, time‑limited offers. Do not target them with full‑price creative — you'll waste the impression. And suppress them from your "full price" retargeting pool so you don't train the full‑price audience to expect discounts.

7. The post‑purchase abandoner

Behaviour: completed a purchase in the past, revisited the site within 60 days and did not buy.

What's going on: they had a good first experience, they came back for a specific reason, something got in the way.

What works: this is the most underused retargeting segment. Send them complementary products to what they bought. Feature new arrivals in their category. Offer a loyalty incentive. The cost of winning back a past customer is a fraction of the cost of acquiring a new one, and the conversion rates show it.

Putting segmentation into practice

You don't need a fancy CDP to do this. You need:

  • A pixel that fires on product view, add‑to‑cart, checkout start, checkout step and purchase
  • A simple rule engine that bucketises sessions by the last event and the session length
  • Distinct creative variants per segment — not 50, just 5–7
  • Suppression logic so a shopper who moves between segments doesn't see contradictory creative

Most retargeting platforms can do this if you configure them. Most brands don't. That's the gap.

The one suppression rule that matters most

Suppress purchasers immediately. Every DSP and push tool has a suppression feature and most programmes have it mis‑configured. Showing a paid retargeting ad to someone who bought the item yesterday is the fastest way to waste your budget and annoy your best customer at the same time.

Check this every Monday. You'd be surprised how often a pixel update breaks it silently.
